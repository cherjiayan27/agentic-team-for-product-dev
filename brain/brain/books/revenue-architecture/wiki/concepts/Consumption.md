# Consumption

## Definition

A [[Monetization Strategy]] in which the buyer pays based on actual usage, an action, or an outcome — not for time-based access. Rightmost position on the arc of the [[Revenue Model]].

## Sub-types (Figure 5.4)

- **Pay-per-use** — charged by unit of consumption (e.g. CPU-seconds, GB of storage, API calls). Cloud infrastructure is the canonical example.
- **Pay-per-action** — charged per event, not per quantity consumed (e.g. pay-per-click ads with CPC ranging cents to tens of dollars)
- **Pay-per-outcome** — charged only when the intended outcome is achieved (e.g. sports-betting affiliate paid on deposit, not on click)

## Characteristics

- **Risk:** seller bears most of it (see [[Risk Shift]]). Seller builds infrastructure before revenue; customer pays only for what they use.
- **Seller objective:** deliver [[Value and Impact|impact]] continuously, often instantaneously
- **Price (1st year):** $100s is typical per customer, dramatically lower than [[Subscription]] or [[Ownership]]
- **[[Win Rate]]:** 1:10 (worse than subscription; low buyer commitment pulls unqualified prospects)
- **[[Revenue Velocity]]:** 1+ day sales cycle; in some cases seconds
- **Retention:** decreases further along the arc

## The low-risk/low-win-rate paradox

Lower buyer risk should intuitively improve conversion, but consumption models see *worse* win rates. Reason: low risk for the buyer means low commitment, which brings a flood of unqualified prospects into the funnel. The conversion rate per prospect drops.

## Trade-offs

- High-volume deals are required to compensate for low per-deal price.
- Inbound-lead-only strategies don't sustain consumption models. Referrals from satisfied customers become critical — which depends on [[Recurring Impact]].

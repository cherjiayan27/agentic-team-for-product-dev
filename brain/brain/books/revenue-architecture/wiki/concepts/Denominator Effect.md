# Denominator Effect

## Definition

As a business's base revenue (the denominator in the growth-rate calculation) grows, the same absolute ARR addition represents a smaller percentage growth. A mathematical inevitability, not a sign of weakness.

## Worked example

- **Year 5:** $100M ARR. Add $10M → 10% growth.
- **Year 6:** $110M ARR. Add the same $10M → 9.1% growth.
- **Year 7:** $120M ARR. Add the same $10M → 8.3% growth.

Absolute growth identical. Percentage growth declining. Untrained observers call this "stalling."

## Why it matters

- **Mathematically unavoidable.** [[First Principles of Recurring Revenue]] Principle 1 is built on this: "The growth rate naturally decreases."
- **Stakeholder expectations must be managed.** A healthy Grownup at 15% annual growth is not underperforming a Startup at 150% — they're at different places on the [[S-Curve]].
- **Valuation frameworks must adjust.** At $500M ARR, focus shifts from growth rate alone to the [[Rule of 40]] (growth% + profit margin ≥ 40%).

## Paired with Compound Effect

The [[Compound Impact]] drives revenue *up*. The Denominator Effect drives the *rate* down. Their combination creates the S-curve shape — exponential when the base is small, plateau as the base grows.

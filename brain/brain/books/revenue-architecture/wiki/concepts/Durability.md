# Durability

## Definition

Growth that **persists because customers keep receiving recurring impact**. The 11th [[12 Revenue Breakpoints|revenue breakpoint]] and the final [[GTM Maturity Stages|maturity stage]] for a GTM motion. Where [[Scalability]] asks "can we repeat it?" and [[Sustainability]] asks "can we afford it?", Durability asks: **"will the customers we've won keep succeeding?"**

## Why it's the endgame

- **Acquisition gets harder over time** (TAM saturation, [[Denominator Effect]])
- **Retention and expansion compound** — see [[Net Revenue Retention]] nonlinearity
- **Durable customers create referrals** that reduce future acquisition costs
- **Brand durability** compounds all of the above

## What durability requires

1. **ICP discipline.** Sellers are selective about which customers commit. Not every qualified opportunity is a good customer — if they can't achieve [[Recurring Impact]], they'll churn and damage reputation.
2. **Qualification by impact potential.** A discovery/priority process that filters based on whether the customer can actually achieve the promised outcome.
3. **Post-sale investment.** Onboarding, success, product teams that deliver impact consistently.
4. **Product-market fit at the *customer level*.** Not just PMF in aggregate — PMF per customer cohort and segment.

## KPIs (from GTM Maturity Stages)

- **[[Gross Revenue Retention]]** — 95%+ annual
- **[[DAU]] / MAU usage depth** — customers actually using the product
- **NPS / CSAT** — qualitative durability signals (not the primary KPI but useful)

## Durability ≠ just retention

Retention is keeping customers; durability is keeping customers *who continue to succeed*. A customer retained only because they can't switch is a future churn risk. A customer retained because they're achieving compounding impact is the foundation of durable growth.

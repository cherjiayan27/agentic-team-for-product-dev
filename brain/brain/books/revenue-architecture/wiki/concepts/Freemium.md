# Freemium

## Definition

A [[Monetization Strategy]] variant sitting at the far end of the [[Revenue Model]] arc. The seller provides a basic version of the product at no monetary cost to the buyer. Monetization happens indirectly — through data, advertising, upsell to a paid tier, or other non-direct exchange.

## Characteristics

- **Price:** $0 at the free tier
- **[[Win Rate]] (free → paid conversion):** typically 1:100. Exceptional products (Spotify, Slack) achieve 1:5.
- **[[Revenue Velocity]]:** minutes — signup to activation is near-instantaneous
- **Retention:** lowest across all monetization strategies; easy to leave

## Why free → paid conversion is so low

With almost no commitment required, a large share of signups are unqualified prospects who will never convert. The signal-to-noise ratio in the funnel drops sharply.

## When freemium makes sense

- **As a market-entry strategy** — let customers self-qualify through [[Product-Led Growth]] (experience impact before commercial commitment)
- **When product virality / network effects exist** — each user increases the product's value to others
- **When indirect monetization paths are strong** — ads (Google), data (many consumer apps), paid-tier upsell (Slack, Notion)

## Failure mode: freemium → paid transition

Among the **hardest transitions** in monetization strategy (§5.3.2). Users psychologically view "free" as a right; introducing fees on previously-free functionality causes severe backlash. Jacco cites the June 2023 Reddit third-party-app-fee episode as an example.

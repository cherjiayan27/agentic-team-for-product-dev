# Hypergrowth

## Definition

The nonlinear growth regime in which annual revenue growth exceeds ~40% sustained over multiple years. Not a larger version of normal growth — a qualitatively different regime driven by compounding [[Net Revenue Retention]] above 1 in the retention/expansion domain of the [[Bowtie]].

## The engine

Powered by the nonlinearity of [[Net Revenue Retention]]. As NRR rises from 1.00 → 1.10 → 1.15 → 1.20 → 1.25, Figure 7.10 shows the compound growth curve becomes **increasingly disproportionate** over time. At NRR = 1.25 over 10 years, the cumulative compounding is dramatic.

Jacco's phrasing: "The faster it goes, the more it accelerates — like a snowball rolling down a hill."

## Conditions required

1. **NRR > 1** — the expansion engine is actually working (upsells, cross-sells, price increases exceed churn and contraction)
2. **Time** — compounding needs years to express itself (see [[First Principles of Recurring Revenue]] Principle 3)
3. **Stable retention** — churn stays below expansion; otherwise NRR flips below 1 and the nonlinearity works *against* you
4. **Acquisition is not the constraint** — but after ~$10M ARR, acquisition is no longer the engine anyway (see [[Four Stages of Growth]])

## Why it's misunderstood

- **Linear intuition fails.** A leader unfamiliar with nonlinearity underestimates the effect of a 5-percentage-point NRR improvement.
- **Attribution is confusing.** Revenue 3 years from now depends heavily on what happens in customer success *today*. Acquisition teams get credit; post-sales teams get cut first when budgets tighten.
- **[[Volatility]] — the flip side.** If NRR drops below 1, the same nonlinearity that produced hypergrowth now drives accelerated decline. The 2022 SaaS downturn is Jacco's cited example.

## What unlocks it

- Dedicated [[Expansion]] team (not the sales team doing renewals; see [[Retention]] risk section)
- [[Recurring Impact]] delivery at scale
- Breaking down silos between acquisition, onboarding, success, and expansion — the [[Bowtie]] operates as a system
- Marginal gains compounding: 10% better at every stage instead of 100% better at one stage

# Man in the Hole

## Definition

Kurt Vonnegut's classic story arc, adapted in Revenue Architecture as the narrative framework for SPICED-based sales storytelling. Three parts: Situation → Pain + negative Impact (the falling) → positive Impact after solving it (the ascent). The emotional contrast between the lows and the highs makes the story memorable.

## The three parts mapped to SPICED

| Part | Story beat | SPICED element |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Situation** | Similar customer context | **S**ituation |
| **2. Falling** | Pain + worsening negative Impact from inaction | **P**ain + negative **I**mpact |
| **3. Ascent** | Solution taken; positive Impact for person + company | Positive **I**mpact (both Rational and Emotional) |

## Rocky Balboa as the canonical example

- **Part 1 (Situation)** — Rocky Balboa, debt-collecting boxer, passion doesn't match unfulfilled life
- **Part 2 (Pain)** — Faces Apollo Creed, outmatched, endures a punishing bout to his lowest point
- **Part 3 (Impact)** — Doesn't clinch victory, but earns respect and admiration — becomes an icon of resilience

His ascent from hardship to the brink of success embodies the arc's emotional structure.

## Why it works in sales

Vonnegut's insight: **lows make highs feel higher**, which makes stories memorable. Sales teams using Man in the Hole narratives:

- Become more relatable (empathy through similar situation)
- Create emotional engagement (the falling)
- Deliver the positive Impact with more weight
- Are easier to recall by customers (who retell the story internally to the Decider)

## How to use

Marketing creates **codified customer stories** using the arc. Sales teams then adapt and share them consistently. Not 50 stories — a small set of well-crafted arcs, swapped by industry/persona.

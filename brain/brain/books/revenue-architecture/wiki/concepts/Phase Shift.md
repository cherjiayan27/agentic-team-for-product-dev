# Phase Shift

## Definition

A transformational change in a company's [[Monetization Strategy]], [[Revenue Model]] position, or fundamental growth model. Not a pivot, not a campaign — a multi-year rebuild that touches products, services, roles, technology, data, reporting, and culture.

## Distinguishing properties

- **Years, not quarters.** Adobe's perpetual → subscription transition took 3+ years (2011–2014 for revenue mix to flip; profitability didn't return until 2016).
- **Cultural shift is primary.** Product and commercial are downstream of it.
- **Severe failure penalty.** High cost, significant revenue loss, wasted time measured in years.
- **Change of executives during the shift is highly disruptive.** See also the [[Chief Revenue Officer]] short-tenure trap — a CRO change mid-Phase Shift typically resets the clock.

## Four common triggers (Figure 5.16)

1. **Perpetual → Subscription** (e.g. Adobe) — most iconic shift
2. **Subscription → Consumption** — usually to enable larger lead flow at lower price points
3. **Consumption → Subscription** — to secure larger deal sizes
4. **Freemium → Paid** — among the **hardest** transitions; users psychologically treat free as a right. Reddit's June 2023 third-party-app-fee controversy is the recent canonical example.

## The Adobe case study (verbatim)

- **2011:** Adobe Creative Suite perpetual business = $3.4B revenue, 97% gross margin
- **Decision:** transition to SaaS despite strong perpetual business
- **Cost:** cultural, technological, sales/marketing redesign. Initial losses.
- **2014:** subscription revenue $2.1B vs. license revenue $1.6B — inflection point
- **2016:** turnaround
- **2022:** $150B valuation on $17B ARR (8.8x multiple)

## Rules

- **Don't disguise a Phase Shift as a marketing campaign** or a sales methodology change. The magnitude is transformational, not tactical.
- Treat it as a capital-intensive, multi-year programme. Align the board and executive team to the timeline before starting.

# Subscription

## Definition

A [[Monetization Strategy]] in which the buyer pays a recurring fee to use a product/service over an agreed period, without owning the asset. The middle position on the arc of the [[Revenue Model]]. The dominant monetization strategy in modern B2B SaaS — over 30,000 companies built in the past decade.

## Sub-types (Figure 5.3)

- **Monthly SaaS** — pay per month; highest flexibility, lowest retention (~78% annualised)
- **Annual SaaS** — pay per year; >90% retention, standard B2B SaaS default
- **Multi-year SaaS (2+ years)** — >95% retention; high commitment; forgoes some compound-growth mechanics; common when deep onboarding/training is involved or when budgets force full-year spend

As the billing period lengthens, the model trends toward [[Ownership]]. As it shortens (monthly → weekly → hourly), it trends toward [[Consumption]].

## Characteristics

- **Risk:** balanced, with the seller taking on more than in ownership (see [[Risk Shift]]). Seller commits to product development, infrastructure, and ongoing value delivery.
- **Seller objective:** deliver [[Value and Impact|impact]] (not just promise value)
- **Price (1st year):** $10K+ for annual B2B SaaS (varies widely); $1K+ for monthly
- **[[Win Rate]]:** 1:5 annual, 1:6–7 monthly
- **[[Revenue Velocity]]:** ~30+ days sales cycle for annual SaaS; ~10 days for monthly
- **Retention:** >90% annual, >78% monthly (see also [[Retention]])

## Why retention drops on monthly

Four reasons (§5.2.6):
1. **Flexibility** — easier to cancel
2. **Commitment** — less upfront investment from buyer
3. **Price** — monthly contracts are typically 10–50% more expensive per unit time; customers are price-sensitive
4. **Engagement** — lower buyer commitment correlates with lower product engagement

## Dependency on recurring impact

Subscription only works if [[Recurring Impact]] is delivered continuously. See [[First Principles of Recurring Revenue]] Principle 5: Recurring Revenue = Recurring Impact.

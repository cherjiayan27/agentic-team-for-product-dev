# Three Es

## Definition

Three lenses for evaluating business operations, stacked onto the [[Six-Layer Model Stack]]. Effectiveness (scalable), Efficiency (sustainable), and Economy (durable) — together they form the informed-decision framework for evaluating GTM maturity.

## The three

| E | Meaning | Linked to |
|---|---|---|
| **Effectiveness** | Did we achieve the goal? | [[Scalability]] — growth via velocity |
| **Efficiency** | Did we achieve it with minimal wasted resources? | [[Sustainability]] — cost-efficient growth |
| **Economy** | Did we optimise resource use for the best quality outcome? | [[Durability]] — high-quality, impact-delivering growth |

## Mapped to the stack (Figure 3.5)

- [[GTM Model]] → Effectiveness (Scalable, Velocity)
- [[Growth Model]] → Efficiency (Sustainable, Cost)
- [[Operating Model]] → Economy (Durable, Quality)

Lower static layers (Mathematical / Data / Revenue Models) feed the evidence base.

## Why they're sequenced this way

- **Effectiveness** comes first — without growth, nothing else matters
- **Efficiency** comes second — once growth works, cost structure must be managed
- **Economy** comes last — durable, high-quality outcomes produce the compounding recurring revenue advantage

This sequence mirrors the [[GTM Maturity Stages]] (Scalability → Sustainability → Durability) and the [[12 Revenue Breakpoints]] progression.

## In practice

For any initiative:

1. **Effectiveness check** — does the initiative move the top-line metric?
2. **Efficiency check** — what's the cost per unit of output?
3. **Economy check** — does this improve long-term quality (NRR, GRR, DAU) or trade it off for short-term?

Initiatives that pass only #1 (grow at all costs) are the Golden Era pattern. Initiatives that pass #1+#2+#3 are the sustainable-growth future.

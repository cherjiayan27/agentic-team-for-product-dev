# Unit Economics

## Definition

Shorthand for the outputs of a [[Growth Formula]], normalised to a single commit or a single unit of revenue. Lets a revenue leader express the cost and conditions of growth in a one-line statement.

## Canonical form

> "To create $X in revenue, we need Y leads per month."

Fully expanded (Jacco's Exercise 7.5):

> - To create ____ in revenue, we will need ______ leads per month.
> - It will take ____ leads to turn into ______ opportunities.
> - Then it takes _____ opportunities to gain the commitment on one deal.
> - Each deal, on average, brings us _____ in [[Annual Recurring Revenue|ARR]].
> - If we sustain our [[Net Revenue Retention|NRR]] over the next 5 years, the [[Lifetime Value|LTV]] per customer is ___________.

## Worked example (from Table 7.3)

> 253 visitors → 30 leads → 5 opportunities → 4 qualified → 1 win at $18,408 ARR → $106,909 LTV over 5 years at the given NRR profile.

## Why it matters

- **Planning.** Quickly derives headcount, marketing spend, campaign targets from a revenue goal.
- **Scenario testing.** Change one metric, rerun the unit economics, see the LTV impact. See [[Scenario Analysis]].
- **Cross-team alignment.** Marketing, sales, and success can be coordinated around a common set of numbers that map back to the same underlying [[Growth Formula]].

## Rules

- **One set of unit economics per GTM motion.** Different motions have different ratios.
- **Normalize.** Sales price = list × (1 − discount). Measure win rate against qualified opportunities.
- **Review per cohort.** Metrics drift across cohorts as product, market, and team change.

## Relationship to sustainability

Scalability (from Ch 7) measures how the system responds to adjustments. Sustainability adds **cost** to the picture — covered in Ch 10 ([[GTM Model]]). A motion with great unit economics on paper may not be sustainable if its cost structure is hidden in the formula.

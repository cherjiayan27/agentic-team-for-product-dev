# Critical Event Timeline

## Definition

A reverse-engineered sequence of time-bound milestones anchored on the day the **customer** achieves first Impact — not the day the seller gets the contract signed. From Daniel J. Adams (Ariba Software). Abbreviated CET.

## The anchor principle

Most GTM teams plan forward from "Let's close this quarter." The CET inverts that:

- **Start with first Impact date** — when will the customer actually receive value?
- **Work backward** — what must be true for first Impact on that date?
- **Identify the time-critical [[Critical Event]]s** along the way

## Example (Table 8.1)

| Event | Date | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| First Impact | Oct 12 | Annual all-hands meeting where the new capability demos |
| Deployment and Testing | Sept 29 | Installation, training, testing |
| Mutual Commitment | Sept 15 | Executed paperwork |
| Legal Review | Sept 1 | Draft contract into legal review |
| Scope Sign-off | Aug 22 | Stakeholder agreement on scope |

The deal closes Sept 15. But the *critical* anchor is Oct 12. If that date slips, the deal re-prioritises.

## Properties

- **Anchors on customer Impact**, not seller contract
- **Starts from first Impact and works backward**
- **Stays focused on a handful of tasks** — usually 4–6 key milestones
- **Confirmed via short standalone email** — useful later if delay disputes arise

## Why it matters

- **Forecast accuracy.** Deals with a confirmed CET close on time far more reliably than deals tracked purely by seller-side stages.
- **Escalation clarity.** If a buyer-side task slips (e.g. legal review), the CET tells you which downstream events shift.
- **Alignment with the Decider.** The CET is effectively the Decider's internal project plan. Co-owning it makes the seller a partner, not a vendor.

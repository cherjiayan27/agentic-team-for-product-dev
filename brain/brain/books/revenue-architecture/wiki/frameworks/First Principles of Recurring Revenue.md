# First Principles of Recurring Revenue

## Definition

Jacco's five foundational truths about how a recurring revenue engine actually behaves. Derived by applying first-principles thinking to subscription businesses, in contrast to operating them as if they were perpetual-sale businesses.

## The five principles

1. **The growth rate naturally decreases.** As ARR grows, maintaining the same percentage growth rate becomes mathematically harder. A $2M annual increase represents 100% growth at $2M ARR but only 20% at $10M ARR. This is a natural business progression, not a strategy failure.
2. **[[Retention]] and [[Expansion]] power growth beyond $10M ARR.** [[Acquisition]] dominates early. Around $10M ARR, retention takes over; retention and expansion both follow hyperbolic/polynomial growth curves that exceed acquisition in a healthy business. Expansion typically surpasses acquisition around $100M ARR.
3. **The effects of recurring revenue take time.** Profits compound over years, not quarters. Year 1 typically has zero profit (acquisition cost ~= first-year revenue). Real profitability accrues as retention and expansion compound against their lower ongoing cost ratios.
4. **The [[Risk Shift]] — risk of the purchase shifts from the buyer to the seller.** In a perpetual model the buyer bore the risk post-purchase (installation, operation, failure). In a subscription model the seller bears it (infrastructure, updates, support, continuous delivery of value).
5. **[[Recurring Revenue]] is the result of [[Recurring Impact]].** Customers keep paying only while they keep receiving value. Without continuous value delivery, churn is inevitable. Netflix analogy: stop delivering new content, subscribers leave within months.

## When to use

- Diagnosing why a recurring revenue business is underperforming (usually: it's being operated as a perpetual business).
- Setting long-term strategy — e.g. recognising at $8M ARR that retention will soon matter more than acquisition.
- Framing investor/leadership conversations about why short-term acquisition pushes can harm long-term profitability (Principle 3).
- Arguing against reassigning sales teams to renewals/expansions (Principle 2 + 3).

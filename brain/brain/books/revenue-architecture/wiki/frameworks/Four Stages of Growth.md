# Four Stages of Growth

## Definition

The temporal sequence through which the [[Three Components of Growth]] successively dominate a recurring revenue business's growth curve. Depicted in Jacco's Figure 2.10.

## The four stages

1. **Stage 1 — Acquisition dominates.** Early years. New-customer revenue is the main (often only) growth source. Retention is small because the customer base is small.
2. **Stage 2 — Retention catches up.** Typically around **$10M ARR**, retention starts shadowing acquisition and then surpasses it. Retention's hyperbolic curve overtakes acquisition's linear one.
3. **Stage 3 — Expansion exceeds acquisition.** Typically around **$100M ARR**, ~8 years after reaching the first $1M ARR. Expansion revenue from existing customers dominates new-customer revenue.
4. **Stage 4 — Lifetime extension produces disproportionate profit.** As customer lifetime increases, profit grows disproportionately because retention/expansion costs are much lower than acquisition costs. The "recurring revenue factory" reaches full power.

## When to use

- To anticipate the strategy shift required at different ARR thresholds. A company at $8M ARR should be investing in the retention muscle *before* acquisition slows.
- To challenge short-term thinking that over-invests in acquisition when growth slows (the common misstep — see [[First Principles of Recurring Revenue]] Principle 2 and the [[Chief Revenue Officer]] incentive problem).
- As a benchmark: where is *this* business on the sequence, and what should it be doing to enable the next transition?

## Thresholds (from Jacco)

- **~$10M ARR** — retention should exceed acquisition
- **~$100M ARR** — expansion should exceed acquisition
- **~2 years** — typical time to shift an organisation from acquisition-focused to retention-focused (not just hiring CSMs — requires engineering, product, and go-to-market changes)

# Three Components of Growth

## Definition

Recurring revenue growth decomposes into three distinct components — not one. Each component has its own growth curve, cost structure, and relative dominance across a company's lifetime.

## Components

1. **[[Acquisition]]** — revenue from new customers. Depends on the organisation's ability to *promise* value through its value proposition. High cost (typically ~100% of first-year revenue is acquisition cost).
2. **[[Retention]]** — revenue retained from existing customers through renewal. Depends on the organisation's ability to *deliver* value (see [[Recurring Impact]]). Lower cost (~10% of retained revenue).
3. **[[Expansion]]** — additional revenue from existing customers (upgrades, cross-sell, usage growth). Also depends on delivered value. Intermediate cost (~20% of expanded revenue).

## When to use

- When analysing a subscription business's revenue, always split it into these three. A single "growth rate" number hides which component is actually working.
- Plotting these three components over ≥8 periods as separate (non-stacked) columns reveals the trend that drives strategic decisions (Jacco's Exercise 2.1–2.4, pp. 65–66).
- A healthy recurring revenue business eventually sees retention and expansion dominate acquisition — that's [[Four Stages of Growth]] playing out.

## Key properties

- Growth from acquisition = **promise** of value.
- Growth from retention and expansion = **delivery** of value ([[Recurring Impact]]).
- Each component follows a polynomial/hyperbolic growth curve — a 2nd or 3rd-degree polynomial trendline typically fits with R² > 0.98 (Exercise 2.3).

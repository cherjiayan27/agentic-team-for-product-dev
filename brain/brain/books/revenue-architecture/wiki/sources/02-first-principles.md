# Chapter 02 — First Principles

**Source:** `raw/markdown/02-first-principles.md` (original PDF: `raw/pdf-chapters/02-first-principles.pdf`, book pages 42–69)

Includes Part I introduction (Fundamentals, pp. 42–45) and Chapter 2 proper (pp. 46–69).

## Thesis

Recurring revenue is a fundamentally different engine from perpetual revenue, and most operators mismanage it by running it like a traditional one-off-sale business. By applying first-principles thinking, Jacco derives five foundational truths of recurring revenue that together explain where growth actually comes from, why it slows, and how to operate the engine correctly.

## Key claims

- Growth in a recurring revenue business comes from **three components**, not one: [[Acquisition]], [[Retention]], and [[Expansion]] — see [[Three Components of Growth]].
- Each component follows a distinct growth curve, typically **polynomial/hyperbolic** rather than linear. Acquisition dominates early; around **$10M ARR** retention takes over; around **$100M ARR** expansion exceeds acquisition — see [[Four Stages of Growth]].
- There are 5 [[First Principles of Recurring Revenue]]:
  1. The growth rate naturally decreases as ARR grows.
  2. Retention and expansion power growth beyond $10M ARR.
  3. The effects of recurring revenue take time (years, not quarters).
  4. The [[Risk Shift]] — risk of the purchase moves from buyer to seller under subscription models.
  5. [[Recurring Revenue]] is the result of [[Recurring Impact]].
- Growth from acquisition depends on **promising value**; growth from retention and expansion depends on **delivering value** — i.e. on [[Recurring Impact]].
- Reassigning the sales (acquisition) team to handle renewals and expansions causes acquisition-level costs (~100% of revenue) to replace retention-level costs (~10%), collapsing long-term profitability. Tied to the common short-tenure [[Chief Revenue Officer]] incentive to hit short-term targets.
- Two market trends follow from the [[Risk Shift]]: quality-over-quantity outreach, and [[Product-Led Growth]] (self-qualifying via early impact).
- Foreshadow: models and data (Ch 3) let us design this engine; systems and processes (Ch 4) let us operate it.

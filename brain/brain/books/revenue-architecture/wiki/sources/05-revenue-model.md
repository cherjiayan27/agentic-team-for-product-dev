# Chapter 05 — The Revenue Model

**Source:** `raw/markdown/05-revenue-model.md` (original PDF: `raw/pdf-chapters/05-revenue-model.pdf`, book pages 121–156)

Includes Part II introduction (Design, pp. 121–123) and Chapter 5 proper (pp. 124–156).

## Thesis

The [[Revenue Model]] — specifically the choice of [[Monetization Strategy]] — is the foundational layer that determines the characteristics of everything that sits on top (data model, mathematical model, operating model, growth model, GTM model — see [[Six-Layer Model Stack]]). Choosing the wrong monetization strategy, mixing multiple strategies, or applying metrics from one strategy to another causes cascading failures throughout the business. Most B2B SaaS companies still operate using perpetual-software instincts despite a subscription revenue model — that mismatch is the root of many common growth problems.

## Key claims

- Part II introduces **three foundational static models** of recurring revenue: [[Revenue Model]], [[Data Model]], [[Mathematical Model]]. On top of these sit three **dynamic models**: [[Operating Model]], [[Growth Model]], [[GTM Model]]. See [[Six-Layer Model Stack]].
- The [[Revenue Model]] is an **arc** of monetization strategies: [[Ownership]] → [[Subscription]] → [[Consumption]], with [[Freemium]] at the far right.
- As you move along the arc (Ownership → Consumption), six characteristics change:
  1. Seller shifts from **selling [[Value and Impact|Value]]** to **delivering [[Value and Impact|Impact]]**
  2. [[Risk Shift]] — risk flips from buyer to seller (reinforces [[First Principles of Recurring Revenue]] Principle 4)
  3. **Price drops by an order of magnitude** per step (perpetual $1M+ → SaaS $10Ks → consumption $100s → freemium $0)
  4. **[[Win Rate]] decreases** (1:3 ownership → 1:5 annual SaaS → 1:6-7 monthly SaaS → 1:10 consumption → 1:100 freemium)
  5. **[[Revenue Velocity]] accelerates for acquisition** (9–18 months → 30+ days → minutes for freemium) but **retention/expansion velocity hinges on concurrent acceleration** of onboarding, adoption, and expansion
  6. **Retention decreases** (100% ownership → >90% annual SaaS → >78% monthly SaaS)
- [[Value and Impact]] are distinct: Value = *promise* of future benefit (the sales pitch); Impact = *fulfilment* of that promise (measurable result during use). Subscription and consumption models hinge on Impact; ownership models hinge on Value.
- Four common mistakes in Revenue Model design:
  1. **Applying the wrong metrics** — using ownership-era 3x pipeline math for a subscription business
  2. **Changing monetization mid-flight** is a [[Phase Shift]] — transformational, takes years (Adobe took 3+ years, 2011–2014, losses before 2016 turnaround)
  3. **Running multiple monetization strategies** confuses customers; recommended: one strategy with at most 3 GTM motions if under $100M ARR
  4. **Mistaking [[Recurring vs Re-occurring Revenue]]** — re-occurring is irregular/unpredictable; recurring is systematic, predictable, and drives higher valuation
- **Adobe case study (Fig 5.14–5.15)** — transitioned from perpetual Creative Suite ($3.4B revenue, 97% gross margin) to subscription SaaS starting 2011. By 2014 subscription revenue ($2.1B) exceeded license revenue ($1.6B). 2016 turnaround. $150B valuation by 2022 on $17B ARR (8.8x multiple).
- Sustainable scale in subscription/consumption cannot be driven by marketing+sales alone — it requires active user/customer involvement in deriving Impact, echoing [[Customer-Centric Growth]].

# Chapter 08 — The Operating Model

**Source:** `raw/markdown/08-operating-model.md` (book pages 233–305) — includes Part III intro

## Thesis

SaaS companies accumulate incompatible **Means and Methods** (tools, methodologies, vocabularies) as they hire, as executives churn, as consultants come and go. By $150M ARR most companies run 12+ GTM motions with exponentially-multiplying interconnections driving cost up and productivity down. The [[Operating Model]] fixes this by combining a **standardised System** (the [[Bowtie]]) with a **standardised Interface** — [[SPICED]] — that lets any Means/Method plug in. SPICED centres the whole organisation on a single constant across the customer journey: **Impact**.

## Key claims

- **Growth from 10 → 100 people** creates the Means-and-Methods explosion. Each new hire imports practices from their previous company. Each tool vendor pushes their own vocabulary. Executives (18-month average tenure at fast-growing companies) each leave a layer of methodology sediment.
- **Interconnections scale superlinearly.** Going from one GTM motion to two doesn't double the complexity — it roughly squares the interconnections between Means/Methods. This is the root cause of the cost/productivity collapse at $20–50M ARR.
- **Operating System analogy.** Market leaders (AWS, Salesforce AppExchange, Adobe Creative Cloud) all run on operating models. The formula: **Apps + Interface + System**. Apply this internally:
  - **System** = the [[Bowtie]] / [[Data Model]]
  - **Apps** = the Means and Methods (BANT, MEDDIC, ABM, ICP, SPIN, NPS, SEO, …)
  - **Interface** = [[SPICED]] (a standardised customer-centric methodology)
- **[[SPICED]] framework** — five elements that form the standardised interface:
  - **S**ituation — existing state
  - **P**ain — the dysfunction in that state
  - **I**mpact — the measurable outcome if Pain is resolved (**the central anchor**)
  - **C**ritical Event — the deadline that creates urgency
  - **D**ecision — how the choice gets made (via the Buying Center)
- **Impact is the one constant** across the entire customer journey (Figure 8.10). Every stage of the Bowtie — from discovery upstream through recurring-impact downstream — centers on the customer's Impact.
- **Two types of Impact:**
  - **Rational Impact** — quantitative, company-level (increase revenue, reduce cost, save time, open new markets)
  - **Emotional Impact** — qualitative, person-level (user experience, job threat, promotion opportunity)
- **Three principles govern Emotional Impact:**
  1. Emotional Impact first benefits the person, then the company (rational is the inverse)
  2. Emotional Impact varies by person (rational is uniform across stakeholders)
  3. Humans make emotional decisions and justify them rationally — which is why product demos matter so much
- **Study data:** reps who uncover Impact during discovery sold **53% more** against the same opportunity pool (Winning by Design, 2023, n=50,000+ opportunities).
- **Rocky Balboa / Man in the Hole narrative structure.** Uses Situation → Pain → Impact as a story arc for memorable sales narratives (Kurt Vonnegut's story arc).
- **Discovery peels the onion.** Situation → Pain → Impact — 3 to 7 questions, using customer's own language. Don't ask "what's the impact?" directly; discover it layer by layer.
- **Critical Event ≠ Compelling Event.** Compelling = carrot (opportunity); Critical = stick (urgency/risk). A renewal date is usually Compelling (service doesn't cut off instantly); a regulatory deadline or security breach is Critical.
- **Priority = f(Impact, Time).** When priority crosses a threshold, Compelling becomes Critical. SaaS priorities fluctuate; budget and ROI don't. GTM teams often miscalibrate.
- **Four priority levels:** Business as Usual → Compelling Event → Critical Event → Mandatory Event.
- **Critical Event Timeline (CET).** From Daniel J. Adams (Ariba). Reverse-engineer the plan starting from the day the *customer* achieves first Impact — not the day the contract is signed.
- **Buying Center.** 9 roles: Initiators, Users, Champions, Deciders, Gatekeepers, Influencers, Executive Buyer, Approvers, Purchaser. Each role maps to different SPICED elements. Roles can overlap in smaller orgs.
- **The decision process** sequences the Buying Center roles around SPICED as a customer progresses through the Bowtie (Figure 8.19).
- **Seller's dual role:** (1) uncover the Decider's rational Impact by addressing the Champion's emotional Impact; (2) equip the Champion to sell the rational Impact internally (the "curiosity gap" technique).

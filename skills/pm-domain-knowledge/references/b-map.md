# B=MAP — BJ Fogg's Behavior Model

**When relevant:** Analyzing why users do or don't take action. Evaluating pain points, root causes, workarounds, success metrics, or solution approaches. Applies across all steps — wherever human behavior is in question.

**Source:** "Tiny Habits: The Small Changes That Change Everything" by BJ Fogg (2019)

---

## The Formula

```
Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt
```

All three must converge at the same moment for a behavior to occur. Even high motivation won't produce action if ability is low or there's no prompt.

## Motivation

Does the user actually want the outcome?

- Don't manufacture fake motivation. Identify users who already have it — they're your best early adopters.
- If you have to convince users to want the outcome, the pain isn't real.
- Motivation is volatile — it spikes and crashes. Don't build products that depend on sustained high motivation.

## Ability

How easy or hard is the behavior to do at that moment. Not about skill or talent — about **perceived effort**.

5 factors that affect how easy something feels:

| Factor | Question | If Hard → |
|---|---|---|
| **Time** | Does it take too long? | Users abandon |
| **Money** | Is it too costly? | Users defer |
| **Physical effort** | Is it too tiring? | Users avoid |
| **Mental effort** | Is it too complex? | Users give up |
| **Routine fit** | Does it clash with what they already do? | Users resist |

If 3+ factors are hard, adoption will fail regardless of motivation.

**Key insight:** Ability is inversely related to motivation. If motivation is high enough, people push through low ability. But if you want reliable, repeatable behavior, make the action as easy as possible rather than relying on motivation.

## Prompt

Does something trigger the behavior at the right moment?

- **External prompts:** push notification, email, in-app nudge, CTA
- **Internal prompts:** user lands on a page, completes a related task, feels the pain

A prompt shown when motivation or ability is low is just noise — or worse, annoying. Timing matters enormously. The same prompt at the wrong moment fails.

---

## How to Apply B=MAP

### In Pain Point analysis (Step 1)

Ask: is the user's pain a motivation problem, an ability problem, or a prompt problem?
- "Users don't use feature X" → is it because they don't want to (M), it's too hard (A), or they don't know it exists (P)?

### In Root Cause analysis (Step 2)

Ask: why does the current workaround fail?
- Map the workaround against the 5 ability factors
- A workaround that requires high mental effort will always leak users

### In End Goal + Metric (Step 3)

Ask: does the success metric measure whether B=MAP converges?
- If the metric is "adoption rate" — that's measuring whether the prompt worked
- If the metric is "retention" — that's measuring whether ability was high enough to sustain behavior
- If the metric is "activation" — that's measuring whether motivation + ability + prompt aligned at first use

### In Methods / Solutions (Step 4)

Score each solution approach on B=MAP:
- **Motivation:** Does the target user already want this?
- **Ability:** Rate each of the 5 factors (time, money, physical, mental, routine)
- **Prompt:** What triggers this at the right moment? Natural or forced?

The approach where all three converge naturally is the winner.

---

## Common B=MAP Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| Building features that require sustained high motivation | Motivation is volatile — it always drops |
| Adding more prompts to fix low adoption | If ability is low, more prompts = more annoyance |
| Making the product powerful but complex | High ability cost kills even high-motivation users |
| Ignoring routine fit | If it doesn't fit into what users already do, they won't adopt it |
| Relying on onboarding to teach ability | Users forget. Make the action inherently simple instead. |
